Archaea

Archaea are unicellular prokaryotic organisms similar to bacteria, but with distinct genetic and biochemical characteristics. They are often found in extreme environments.

Archaea are single-celled organisms without a true nucleus. Their cell walls do not contain peptidoglycan, which distinguishes them from eubacteria. Archaea include extremophiles that thrive in environments such as hot springs, salty lakes, acidic waters, and deep-sea hydrothermal vents. They can be autotrophic, producing their own food, or heterotrophic, consuming organic material.

Although Archaea were once thought to be bacteria, they are now recognized as a separate domain of life. They play important roles in ecosystems, including nitrogen cycling and methane production. Studying Archaea helps scientists understand evolution, the adaptability of life, and potential applications in biotechnology.
